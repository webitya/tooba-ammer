01. What this demonstrates

## Four competencies on display.

01 Strategic foresight Reading colour shifts across fashion, automotive, and lifestyle a year ahead of the factory calendar, and translating them into a range that ships on time.

02 Systems thinking Building a colour matrix — shell, wheel, beading, zipper — that turns every palette decision into a combinatorial system, not a per-SKU choice.

03 CMF discipline Specifying each tone against material (PP/PC), finish (matt/glossy), and emotional intent — so the colour you direct is the colour the factory makes.

04 Commercial translation Tying every colour to a strategic job — shelf attention, emotional resonance, or digital storytelling — so the palette earns its place at retail, not just on the moodboard.

02. The premise

## Colour is competitive advantage, not decoration.

By 2024, Safari's range had narrowed into four colour territories — *black, blue, grey, and teal.* Reliable on volume. Recognisable on shelf. And, increasingly, a problem.

Two signals were arriving from the field at the same time. Dealer feedback came in from every market visit: *the same request, every time — new colours.* The trade was telling us the existing palette was tapped out. They needed reasons to refresh shelf, reasons to call a buyer back, reasons to pitch a Safari range against the brighter, more expressive competitors arriving in their stores.

The second signal was the consumer the trade was reacting to: a younger buyer who had stopped choosing luggage the way her parents had. She wasn't picking a black suitcase because it was practical; she was picking an orange one because it said something. And the brands she was picking weren't Safari — they were the ones offering eye-catching, expressive colour. *We were losing her on the colour wall, before she ever read the spec sheet.*

The category-level truth was that hardshell luggage is largely commoditised. The same polycarbonate shells, the same wheel systems, the same trolley mechanisms move through dozens of brands. *Once shape and size are set, colour is the choice the consumer is actually making.* Safari was still treating it as a finishing decision.

Color Quest started from one position: *if colour is the choice the customer is making, the brand has to direct it deliberately, season on season, with the same rigour we apply to form.*

03. The constraint

## Eighteen developments, one factory's bandwidth.

The brief said "refresh the palette." The factory said "not all at once."

The real constraint on Color Quest wasn't creative budget — it was dye-development bandwidth. Each new shell colour is a substantial undertaking for the manufacturing partner: pigment calibration, sample cycles, batch-to-batch consistency tests, and final sign-off. And critically, *polypropylene and polycarbonate don't share a master batch.* The same colour identity has to be developed twice if it's wanted in both materials — two separate pigment formulations, two calibration cycles, two sign-offs — because the chemistry of the resins reads the same pigment differently.

So "eighteen new colours" isn't eighteen distinct identities. It's eighteen development efforts: ten PP master batches and eight PC master batches, with some colours represented in both materials and counted in each. *The work isn't the colour. It's the colour in the material.*

I phased these eighteen developments across two priorities. The split wasn't about importance; it was about *perceptual impact per development slot.*

P1 High-saturation colours in new family territories Colour families Safari had never offered — the saturated tones that would visibly change how the range read on shelf. Developed first, because the perceptual shift from a new family is larger than the shift from a new tone within an existing one.

P2 New tones within existing colour families Extensions and refinements of families already in the Safari range — lower risk, faster to calibrate against known reference points, and able to ride on the visibility the P1 colours had already opened up at retail.

The discipline was sequencing: don't spend the factory's first cycle on safe extensions; spend it on the colours that change the consumer's read of the brand. Safe extensions get the second cycle, when the shelf has already moved.

04. Designing the workflow

## Before the colours, the pipeline.

The deck on the page exists because of a less visible piece of work that happened first: *I redesigned how the colour development effort itself was structured.*

Before any colour can be developed at the factory, the proposal has to clear an internal gate — stakeholder review with PMs and brand leadership, who decide whether to commit factory capacity to the direction. The deck that goes into that room is the artefact this workflow exists to produce. *Get the deck wrong and the colours never get made.*

When I took ownership of Color Quest, the starting state of the pre-approval work was three streams running simultaneously — trend research and Pantone mapping, screen and master-file calibration, and visualisation prep for stakeholder presentation. Everyone touched every stage. No one owned any of them. The output was duplicated effort, broken handoffs, and colour proposals that arrived at stakeholder review without a finished brief behind them. *The system was failing before it produced anything.*

I redesigned the workflow before I touched a swatch. The work had to move in one direction, not three — each stage owned by the capability best suited to it, each handing a clean, signed-off output to the next.

01 Research and direction Competitive mapping, global trend reads, market visits, sell-through analysis, Pantone reference selection. The stage I owned end to end — feeding calibration with a fully briefed colour direction, not an unfinished thought.

02 Screen and master-file calibration Pantone-to-screen calibration, master colour file maintenance. Produces the canonical reference each downstream stage works against — the single source of truth between intent and manufacture.

03 Visualisation and presentation layout The stakeholder deck itself — layout, sequence, visual hierarchy. I owned this end to end alongside research and direction, working with the broader development team for component renders and asset prep. The deck is what earns the development sign-off; designing it as a sales document, not a working file, was the difference between approval and rework.

My title was Design & Development Manager — but the other two on this work weren't my direct reports. The work happened because the structure made the right thing easy and the wrong thing visible, not because anyone was assigned to me. *The deck is the artefact. The pipeline is the work.*

What happens after the deck is approved — the actual factory handoff, the Pantone specification, the master-batch development — is a separate discipline. That work is covered later in the case study.

05. The framework

## Every tone earns its place three ways.

The core of the system is a framework I built to stop colour decisions becoming taste arguments. Every tone in the palette has to do at least one of three jobs, and the strongest do all three.

01 Shelf impact Attention magnets — papaya, lime sorbet, butter yellow, caramel. The colours that pull a buyer across the aisle from ten feet.

02 Emotional resonance Plant-toned greens — avocado, peppermint, seaweed — that tap the subconscious comfort people associate with nature. The colours that close a sale at arm's length.

03 Digital storytelling Pastels and botanical tones that photograph cleanly — built for e-commerce thumbnails, Instagram grids, and mood-driven marketing. The colours that earn the second look online.

*Each tone has a role: shelf impact, emotional resonance, and digital storytelling.*

06. Reading the year

## Trend, translated into a luggage decision.

A colour direction is only as good as the read it sits on. Each season of Color Quest opens with trend trees — colour families pulled from automotive, footwear, fashion, and interiors, then tested against what's actually possible in PP and PC at scale. The discipline is the translation, not the trend-spotting.

01 Sage Green Family A versatile, soothing tone surfacing across fashion, footwear, and automotive — Hyundai Exter, New Balance 574, sportswear collections, premium interiors. The dominant earthy-calm green of the season.

02 Aquatic Blue · Skyway Blue A blue-green family read as the emotional escape trend evolving out of post-pandemic wellness palettes. Designed to evoke sophistication and depth rather than the holiday-led blues luggage typically defaults to.

03 Ray Flower — mustard A warm yellow showing up across luxury automotive, footwear, and tech accessories. Anchored as one of the season's most consistent accents across the mood territories.

07. The recalibration

## When the moodboard out-ran the factory.

Early exploration leaned on the trend forecasts directly — the saturated jewel end the category was moving toward. On paper, the direction was right.

Then the samples came back. *The shells looked premium. The rest of the trolley did not.* The in-house factory in Halol, building for the Indian core market at the Safari cost structure, couldn't deliver components at the finish level a jewel-toned shell visually demanded. A premium shell wearing economy fittings doesn't read premium; it reads disappointing.

I recalibrated toward softer, more contemporary tones — colours the whole product could sustain. *The colour the consumer sees has to be a colour the whole trolley can carry.*

08. The system

## A colour matrix, not a palette.

Every hardshell trolley is made up of four touchpoints that can each carry colour independently: *shell, wheel, beading (the rivet line), and zipper.* Most brands set the shell colour and let the others default to black. I built the matrix so every season has a finite, validated set of combinations across all four. I costed each combination, signed off the component pairings, and handed the range planner a buildable set rather than a palette to interpret.

01 More distinctive SKUs from fewer colours A palette of multiple tones, combined across four touchpoints, generates dozens of distinctive shelf identities — without the cost of new shell tooling each time.

02 Faster range expansion Once a combination is approved, it can be repeated across cabin, medium, and large in one decision — not three sampling cycles.

10. From swatch to shell

## The colour you direct is not the colour the factory ships.

*Once the deck cleared internal approval, the factory handoff began — and a different discipline took over.* The pre-approval work was about building the case for a direction. The post-approval work was about translating that direction into colours that would actually arrive on the shell at the saturation, finish, and tone the brand had signed off.

Specifying a colour is not the same as receiving it. Polycarbonate shell forming stretches the material as it takes the mould; the colour pulls lighter and the white base under the pigment dilutes the saturation. *The Pantone you hand in and the colour out of the mould are never the same tone* — that's not factory failure, it's how the process works. And because PP and PC don't share a master batch, the factory receives the brief twice for any colour wanted in both materials — a PP Pantone list and a PC Pantone list, developed in parallel as two separate master-batch programmes.

I designed two manufacturing decisions into the brief to close the spec-to-shell gap before it opened:

01 Spec one tone warmer than the target The Pantone code given to the factory is intentionally a notch deeper or warmer than the finished colour the consumer should see. The forming process will lift it back.

02 Two Pantone codes per colour, not one Each direction is briefed with a pair of close codes rather than a single target. The factory samples both; the team picks the closer match in one cycle instead of asking for a resample. Built-in optionality upstream costs nothing; resampling downstream costs the launch window — typically two to three months from factory request to validated sample.

11. Reviving existing shells

## New tooling was never the answer.

The most commercially direct test of the system: three existing shell platforms that had plateaued on e-commerce, reading dated and undifferentiated against a market that had moved on. Color Quest answered not "what should the new shell look like?" but *"what would it take to make the existing ones look new?"* In each case, the answer was colour and combination — nothing else.

Colour-led revival has commercial logic that a new shell can't match: *no new tooling cost, faster speed to market, and the new SKU inherits the listing reviews, search rankings, and retailer-relationship equity of the platform it sits on.* A new shell would need eighteen months to earn what a re-colouring inherits on day one.

Astra → Astra Neo New colourway, same shell. The Neo reads as a different product on the listing page.

Thorium → Thorium Royale · Thorium Neo Two new colourway expressions from one shell tool. The same hard geometry now reads current in the new palette.

Pentagon → Pentagon Pro New colour and component combination. Earns a new product name, a new listing, and a new range position — with zero tooling investment.

12. The result on shelf

## What the range looks like now.

The same palette appeared across all three of Safari's channels — trade, e-commerce, and hypermarket — reviving plateaued shell platforms as new products and giving the brand one recognisable colour language across every surface a consumer encountered it on. The clearest evidence is the live search result — the Safari range as a consumer sees it today.

The original Safari staples — black, charcoal, dark navy — sit alongside Astra Neo, Thorium Neo, Pentagon Pro, and Thorium Royale. The range reads as varied, current, and expression-led. It no longer reads as economy-coded.

*The palette the consumer sees today is the direct output of the direction system.* The shells are the same shells. The tooling is the same tooling. The difference is colour, combination, and the decision to treat both as a strategic system rather than a finishing choice.

13. Reflection

## What this approach changes.

Color Quest's claim is specific: it turns colour from a per-SKU argument into a direction system that compounds. Each season inherits the framework, evolves the trees, and produces a tighter, faster, more distinctive range — without new tooling, without new forms, and without the resampling loops that come from under-specified colour briefs.

The shell revival results are the most commercially direct proof of that. Three existing platforms were relaunched as new product on every platform, with reviews, search rankings, and listing credibility that a genuinely new SKU would need eighteen months to build. *The range looked new because the colour system was new — not because the products were.*

What travels is the method: a three-role test, a combinatorial matrix, a per-colour CMF brief, a manufacturing-aware Pantone specification, a sequential workflow with each stage owned by the capability best suited to it, and the discipline of building each season on top of the last rather than starting from scratch. That approach works in any category where colour is the primary consumer choice and the brand is still treating it as the last decision.
